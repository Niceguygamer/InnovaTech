School Loads and Class Standing

ESICO, BRYAN March 20, 2026 5 min read

System Overload There is a limit to how much information a human brain can process at once. In computer science terms, it is like trying to run a high end game on a machine with limited RAM. Eventually, things start to lag. In education, this is called Cognitive Load Theory. When we are forced to manage too many complex tasks simultaneously, our ability to learn and perform begins to degrade rapidly.

The Yerkes Dodson Law Performance actually improves with a little bit of stress. It keeps us focused and motivated. But once that stress passes an optimal point, performance drops off a cliff. During finals week, many students are pushed far past this point. Stress levels that jump from a 4.5 at the start of the semester to a 9.3 during exams are not productive. They are destructive.

The Performance Paradox

The Meaningful Work Factor Workload feels much heavier when it feels meaningless. Rote busywork and ambiguous assignments generate more stress than difficult, meaningful projects. When we understand the "why" behind a task, our cognitive capacity seems to expand. When we don't, even simple tasks feel like an impossible burden.

Conclusion Optimization requires balance. To achieve genuine excellence, we have to stay within the "optimal arousal zone" and avoid the burnout of chronic overload.

List of Advice

  • Break large projects into smaller, manageable chunks.
  • Use "deep work" sessions to focus on one task at a time.
  • Identify which assignments have the highest learning value.
  • Take frequent, short breaks to prevent cognitive fatigue.
  • Use visual tools like flowcharts to organize complex information.
