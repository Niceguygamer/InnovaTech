Topic # 2

Sleep Quality vs. Quantity

MUNDA, SOFIA ADELAIDE March 20, 2026 5 min read


“A good laugh and a long sleep are the two best cures for anything.” - Irish Proverb

Have you ever slept a full eight hours and still woken up exhausted? Many of us assume that when you have more hours of sleep it automatically means better rest. But the truth is, sleep quality matters more than quantity. Without deep sleep and healthy sleep cycles, even long nights in bed can leave you drained.

The Science of Sleep Quality

Sleep is divided into stages: light sleep, deep sleep, and REM sleep. Deep sleep restores the body, while REM sleep consolidates memory and fuels creativity. If your sleep is fragmented—due to stress, irregular schedules, or late-night screen use—you may spend hours in bed without reaching restorative stages.

Why Quantity Alone Isn't Enough

People often believe that sleeping longer is enough, but in reality it doesn’t guarantee rest. We are more after the numbers but the quality of sleep isn’t there. Poor sleep hygiene—like inconsistent bedtimes, caffeine late in the day, or scrolling on your phone before bed—disrupts sleep cycles. This leads to shallow sleep and morning fatigue. Which is why people still feel very sleepy which also affects their productivity for the day.

Personal Experience: Discovering Sleep Hygiene

I was one of the many people who believed that having eight hours of sleep meant a good rest and would restore my energy back. Not until I noticed that even when I had a full eight hours or more of sleep, I still woke up feeling sleepy and tired. That’s when I realized that I’m not having a good quality of sleep despite having enough quantity. I started changing my habits before going to bed and started doing things like journaling or listening to calm music to prepare my brain for rest. Once I transitioned to these habits, the stress of the day turned into restorative sleep—I finally felt refreshed.

Sleep and Mental Health

Quality sleep is directly tied to mental health. Poor sleep increases anxiety, irritability, and difficulty concentrating. Sleep is not just about hours—it’s about depth and consistency. By prioritizing sleep quality, you can truly allow yourself to recover from a long and tiring day. A good sleep often lies not in how long you sleep, but in how well you sleep.

Top 10 Advice/Tips

  1. Keep a consistent bedtime and wake-up time - Even on weekends.
  2. Limit caffeine after 2 PM - Avoid disrupting deep sleep.
  3. Create a calming bedtime ritual - Read, journal, or meditate.
  4. Keep your room cool, dark, and quiet - Optimize your sleep environment.
  5. Avoid heavy meals before bed - Digestion can interfere with rest.
  6. Put away screens at least 30 minutes before you sleep - Blue light delays melatonin.
  7. Try breathing exercises if you wake up at night - Helps ease back into sleep.
  8. Use white noise or calming sounds - Mask disruptive noises.
  9. Exercise during the day - Physical activity improves sleep quality.
  10. Track your sleep patterns - Use a journal or app to identify habits that help or harm.
