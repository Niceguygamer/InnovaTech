How Tech Programs Push You to the Edge

LEONGSUN, EUWIN March 31, 2026 5 min read

Burnout does not come out of nowhere. In CS and IT programs, there are specific things baked into the structure that make it more likely than in almost any other course. Once you see them, they are hard to unsee.

Tech programs are demanding by design. The material is hard, the projects are real, and the pace does not slow down for anyone. That is part of what makes the degree worth something. But there is a difference between a program being challenging and a program being structured in a way that quietly breaks people down over time.

The Project Stack Problem

Most tech students are not dealing with one heavy subject at a time. They are dealing with five or six simultaneously, and several of those have major project requirements due around the same period. You are not burnt out from one thing. You are burnt out from the accumulation.

Each subject on its own is manageable. But when you have a machine project, a database system, a web app, and a written report all due within the same two-week window, something breaks. Either the quality of everything drops, or you sacrifice sleep, or you sacrifice something else entirely. Usually all three at once.

The Always-On Culture

There is an unspoken expectation in a lot of tech spaces, including college programs, that you should always be doing something. Reading, building, practicing, learning something new. If you are not, you feel like you are falling behind. That feeling does not turn off.

It follows you into weekends, into meals, into the moments when you are supposed to be resting. You sit down to relax and your brain reminds you about that unfinished function or that assignment you have not started. Real rest becomes almost impossible because mentally you are never fully off.

Imposter Syndrome on Top of Everything

A lot of students in tech programs, especially early on, feel like they do not belong. You look at classmates who seem to understand everything immediately, who already know how to code before the course even starts, and you wonder if you are the only one struggling to keep up.

You are not. But that feeling is isolating, and it makes everything harder. You stop asking for help because you do not want to look like you do not know what you are doing. You push yourself harder to compensate. You do not talk about how stressed you are because everyone else seems fine.

Imposter syndrome on top of a heavy workload on top of the always-on culture is a reliable recipe for burnout. Understanding that these pressures exist and are structural, not personal, is the first step to dealing with them without destroying yourself.
