Topic # 5

Building a Life That Does Not Break You

LEONGSUN, EUWIN March 31, 2026 5 min read

Recovering from burnout is one thing. Not burning out again is another. The habits that protect you long term are not dramatic. They are small, consistent, and easy to skip when things get busy. That is exactly why they matter.

The goal here is not to become someone who never feels pressure or stress. That is not realistic in a tech program and honestly probably not even desirable. Some pressure is useful. The goal is to build a baseline that is stable enough that hard stretches do not take you all the way down.

Know What Your Warning Signs Are

Burnout does not arrive all at once. It builds up over weeks, and there are usually early signs that are easy to dismiss if you are not paying attention. For me it was losing interest in the project, getting irritated over small things, and procrastinating on things I normally enjoyed.

Your signs might be different. Maybe it is your sleep pattern shifting. Maybe it is canceling plans consistently. Maybe it is the quality of your work starting to drop without a clear reason. Whatever they are, the earlier you catch them the easier it is to course-correct before it becomes a full burnout situation.

Build in Rest Before You Need It

Most people treat rest as a reward for finishing everything. The problem is that in a tech program there is always something left to finish. If you wait until everything is done to rest, you will never rest. Rest has to be scheduled the same way you schedule work, not as a bonus but as a requirement.

That does not mean large blocks of free time every week. Even small consistent things work. A walk. A meal with no screen in front of you. Time with people you actually like being around. These are not luxuries. They are part of what keeps you functional over the long term.

Be Honest About What You Can Actually Handle

One of the patterns that leads to burnout is consistently overpromising to yourself. You plan more than you can realistically do, fall short, feel bad about it, and then try to compensate by planning even more. It becomes a cycle.

Getting better at estimating what you can actually do in a day or a week, and then planning around that instead of around an ideal version of yourself, makes a real difference. Finishing what you planned feels completely different from finishing less than you planned, even if the actual output is the same.

Things Worth Keeping in Mind

  • Notice the early signs. Irritability, loss of interest, and quality drops usually show up before the crash does.
  • Schedule rest the same way you schedule work. Do not wait until everything is done.
  • Protect at least one thing each week that has nothing to do with school or projects.
  • Sleep is not optional. It is the foundation everything else runs on.
  • Talking about it helps more than most people expect. Find at least one person you can be honest with.
  • Saying no to something is also a decision. It has value. You do not have to justify it every time.
  • You are allowed to do less during the hard weeks. That is not giving up. That is management.

Burnout is not inevitable in a tech program. It is common, but common is not the same as unavoidable. The students who make it through without completely falling apart are not the ones who never struggled. They are the ones who paid attention to themselves, asked for help when they needed it, and built routines that gave them something to come back to when things got hard.

You can do the same. It just takes being honest about what is actually happening before it gets to the point where you have no choice.
