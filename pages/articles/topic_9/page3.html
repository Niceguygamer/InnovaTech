What Burnout Did to My Work

LEONGSUN, EUWIN March 31, 2026 5 min read

The most honest way I can describe burnout is this: I would sit down to work on something I genuinely cared about, open the code, and then just sit there. Not thinking. Not stuck on a problem. Just empty. That was when I knew something was actually wrong.

I had been building Prior for a while by the time burnout really hit. The project had grown a lot. Multiple services, a lot of moving parts, features I was excited about. And then at some point the excitement stopped coming. I still opened the project most days but I was not really there.

The work did not stop. The deadlines did not stop. School requirements piled up on top of the personal projects I was trying to maintain. I kept going mostly on momentum, doing the minimum to keep things from breaking, and telling myself I would feel motivated again soon.

The Quality Drop You Do Not Notice at First

One of the quiet ways burnout shows up in technical work is that your code gets sloppier without you fully realizing it. You start skipping things you would normally do. You stop commenting anything. You skip error handling because you just want the feature to work and move on. You make decisions you know are not great but you are too tired to find the better solution.

I look back at some of the code I wrote during that period and it shows. Not catastrophically, but you can tell it was written by someone who was running low. The logic is messier. The structure does not make as much sense. Things that should have been split up got crammed together because I did not have the energy to do it properly.

When the Thing You Love Starts Feeling Like a Chore

The part that bothered me most was not the quality drop. It was losing the feeling that I actually wanted to be doing this. Programming was something I chose. I started building things before anyone asked me to because it was genuinely fun. When burnout hit, that feeling was just gone.

Opening the editor felt like a task I had to get through, not something I was doing because I wanted to. That shift is hard to describe if you have not felt it, but if you have, you know exactly what I mean. It is not a bad day. It is a sustained flatness that sits on top of everything.

What It Did to Everything Else

Burnout does not stay in one area. When I was burnt out on the work, I was also harder to be around. Less patient. Less present in conversations. Things that would normally roll off me started getting under my skin. My sleep got worse even though I was tired all the time.

It affects your whole life, not just your output. That is part of why it is worth taking seriously. It is not just a productivity problem. It is a you problem, in the sense that the person going through it suffers across the board, not just in their grades or their code.
