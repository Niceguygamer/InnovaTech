Topic # 1

Relationships That Matter

March 20, 2026 5 min read

“You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than you can in two years by trying to get other people interested in you.” — Dale Carnegie

More Than Just Socializing

There is more to college than just lectures, tests, and due dates. It also has to do with the individuals you encounter and engage with on a daily basis. Your entire college experience is shaped by your relationships with classmates, instructors, and group members in ways you might not be aware of right away.

Sometimes the simple fact that you are surrounded by understanding and encouraging people makes work seem easier. Stressful situations can be made more manageable with a helpful teammate, a simple talk, or a shared chuckle. However, even straightforward activities can rapidly become tedious due to poor communication or misconceptions.

You can handle these circumstances more skillfully if you have interpersonal skills. They enable you to reply politely and thoughtfully, communicate your views clearly, and comprehend others better. These abilities are crucial for forming deep connections in daily life in addition to being helpful in the classroom.

In the long term, interpersonal skills might be just as significant as intellectual knowledge. Possessing strong interpersonal skills can improve teamwork, open doors, and make your time in college less stressful and more pleasurable.

Strategies for Making Meaningful Connections

  • Show Genuine Interest:

    As Dale Carnegie suggests, prioritize learning to understand more about your peers rather than simply trying to get them to be interested in you. Asking questions and actively listening is the most engaging way to form new friendships quickly.

  • Enjoy Lectures with Friends:

    Treat relationships as one of the cores of your college experience. Make a conscious effort to engage with your blockmates, group members, or even batchmates, and instructors instead of just focusing solely on lectures and deadlines.

  • Look Beyond the Covers:

    Surround yourself with understanding and encouraging individuals, they will be the people you can trust and rely on. Seek out groupmates and make friends who share positivity, laughter, and helpful conversations that can make stressful academic situations feel more manageable and somewhat fun!

  • Prioritize Clarity:

    Misunderstandings can make the easiest tasks feel tedious. Practice expressing your views clearly and politely, for them to be comfortable to speak their mind, and always strive to fully comprehend the perspectives of others.

  • Invest in Long-Term Skills:

    View every interaction and group project as an opportunity to hone your interpersonal abilities. These skills are just as valuable as intellectual knowledge and will continue to open doors and improve your teamwork long after you graduate. Not every lesson will be found in textbooks; some of the most important skills you'll acquire are those that help you connect with others.
