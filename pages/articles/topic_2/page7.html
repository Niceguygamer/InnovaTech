Topic # 7

Failing Grades vs. The Real World

OBILLO, MON ANDREI March 20, 2026 5 min read

I failed an activity more than once in my college journey. The first time it happened, it felt like the end of the world for me. It felt like the consequences of failing were so devastating, I thought I could never recover from it. Then I failed again. And again. And again. Now, it just seems normal to most of us, and we've grown to accept failure, especially in academic work. I often tell myself, it's okay, grades don't define me. Who cares if I don't get it right the first time? I'll get it right eventually.

One of my most vivid memories of not caring about a failing grade was during one of my classes in my second year, first semester. It was almost the end of the semester and I couldn't care less about what was happening. I was drained and tired, and we had to submit one of our final activities in that subject. I sat in front of my laptop and started, and halfway through, the screen flashed a lot of red lines with all the errors I encountered. It was about an hour before the deadline, and I was sleep-deprived, and so I did what I had to. I just submitted the activity without a care in the world about my score for it. I wanted to sleep, and I prioritized sleep over anything else. I got an all-time low score in that activity, but it was worth it for me.

The Reality of Failing in College vs. the Real World

But, one of the things I have realized is that the real world isn't as forgiving as grades, and we can't exactly say the exact same thing when we fail in real life situations such as failing to meet a deadline for a client or facing a catastrophic failure in a system you made, or even a data leak that could cost millions. It's scary to think how much responsibility lies in your hands as part of the industry. I mean, what am I supposed to do with that? I can't even figure out my own activities right now. In real world situations, I can't just say I don't care and just sleep through it. I actually had to push through and do what I am tasked to do.

One of the things I'm most anxious about in my career is the severity of the consequences of real world jobs. There's no more safeguard against failure, and I can't just do anything again that easily. There are contracts and legal consequences that my feeble mind can't exactly handle as of now. I'm so accustomed to my own coping mechanisms of just letting things be and not stressing myself out with these things that I might just get myself in trouble in the future. In careers such as ours, I think there's too much risk, which I guess is compensated by the salary we might get, but again, it's still unsure, and in previous topics, passion and being happy is still more important.

Collaboration and Support in the Real World

But, one thing I often forget is that in the real world, there's no such thing as being alone. There's networks of people that collaborate and work together to accomplish a lot of things. Throughout my college journey so far, I have been quite blessed with collaborating with a bunch of my classmates, all of whom have skills I don't, and I can safely say that I also got some things that they don't. I guess, we can't avoid the risk that comes with the enormous responsibilities that are placed upon us, but what's great about our field is that we don't have to face everything alone.

Responsibility is an important factor in careers such as ours, and as much as we try to be responsible with what we do, we still need help once in a while. Maybe the reason I often fail activities in school is because of not asking for help when I need to. And I am very sure that the reason why I often succeed is because of the people that are there to help every time I need to. Consequences are scary, but the good news is that we don't have to carry the weight of responsibility alone.

I am still afraid of consequences, but knowing that I won't have to face it alone, that's enough.

Advice

  1. Grades aren't everything; real-world consequences differ.
  2. Accept failure as a normal part of learning and growth.
  3. Prioritize responsibilities and self-care over perfectionism.
  4. Rely on support networks; you don't face challenges alone.
  5. Build resilience and persistence through repeated failure.
