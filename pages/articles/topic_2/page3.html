Topic # 3

The "Everyone is Ahead of Me" Syndrome

OBILLO, MON ANDREI March 20, 2026 5 min read

I feel ashamed sometimes to mention that I'm a computer science major. Why, you may ask? Because I have nothing to show for it. I'm simply carrying that idea, but I can't exactly represent it. If you look up "computer science core" or "pov computer science major", I barely fit the profile. Meanwhile, other students around my year level have done astonishing things and have achieved a lot. Some students are just too far ahead.

Seeing Others Ahead

Funny enough, I do have a groupmate who's also working on this same project, and he's the system programmer of our group. Interestingly, he made an entire platform that now hosts our entire site and has made it possible to develop this entire project, and I think that's so cool. But to be very honest (and I hope he doesn't see this as negative) but I'm quite jealous of his accomplishments so far. To think that I'm even afraid to ask him questions about it because I feel like it might be nonsense or that it's too basic of a question that I shouldn't even be asking (to our SP, I don't think you're hostile or hard to approach, I'm just trying not to seem too noob about these things, even though I totally am). But hey, I am really amazed, just a bit missing out on what he's done for himself already.

Comparisons Everywhere

I also got other classmates that have done a lot more than me, and to think that's not even surprising given the bar isn't so high. One has mastered some tech stacks for web development, one has participated in national level programming competitions, one has joined the university Research and Development Unit, which I might add is something I've wanted to join as well but can't because of my busy schedule and workload. I hate to admit it, but I'm so jealous. What can I even say I've done? I was able to successfully complete my program on simulating memory management algorithms. So did they. I was able to program a polymorphic system for my Object Oriented Programming subject. So did they. I learned git on my own. So did they.

Impostor Syndrome

Impostor syndrome is a huge cause of career anxiety as well, and I'm one of those who have it. I don't think that I'm good enough for my own degree, and that's causing a lot of negativity on myself. Seeing all those students do so well makes me think I might not be for this. I haven't exactly grown out of it, and I think that I'm still not good enough for my own career path. I think most of my reflections and points about career anxiety have a bit of impostor syndrome bleeding into it. I've yet to prove myself wrong as well.

Different Paths

One of the things people say about this is that we do have to accept that everyone's journey is different, and we all have different pacing and capabilities. I have taken pride in being able to quickly learn things, and being able to do things with minimal effort, which has been quite a huge help for my academics in the course of my studies. Not everyone has that, and they all have different strengths and capabilities that they take pride in.

Looking Forward

Another thing I have to accept is that younger me could not even fathom what present me can now do. I used to just think about the endless possibilities and the skills I can develop and gain from taking this major, and even until now I'm still thinking, but of course, with that added firm grasp on the fundamentals and having scaled that huge first step in entering the tech industry, which is to start learning. I am still learning, and will continue to learn, and who knows, maybe in the near future I can also do the same things, or even make my own achievements that people will become jealous of. We don't exactly need to follow the footsteps of others, rather we can follow our own path to things, and I think that's the best way to put it. We'll get there eventually, and maybe even do a lot better things, and it's best to not let that discouraging feeling pull you back from reaching that goal you have.

Where I Stand

I'm still trying to accept all of that, but for now, that's enough.

Advice

  1. Accept that everyone's journey and pace are different.
  2. Don't compare yourself to others; focus on your growth.
  3. Recognize your own unique skills and strengths.
  4. Understand that younger you couldn't have imagined present achievements.
  5. Follow your own path instead of others' footsteps.
