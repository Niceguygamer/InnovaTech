Topic # 2

Wait, Is This All There Is?

OBILLO, MON ANDREI March 20, 2026 5 min read

Module activities, coding problems, simulations, quizzes, and exams. They take up most of my time studying. The countless deadlines, activities that help us understand the ideas, and the projects that give us a glimpse of its practical use. Was it ever enough for me to make my own solutions to real world problems? With what I currently know, I can't even fathom how to make a working system, let alone handle real world situations and real world problems. I mean, is this it? Is it all I'm getting, and am I on my own?

Starting Out

Back in my freshman year, I understood that it was mostly riddled with minor subjects and not much on our majors, which meant I won't really learn much on topics specific to Computer Science. I was able to master programming subjects and dive into Python and Java back then, and was confident with the syntax and basic programming concepts. Then came second year, which was full of major subjects and more theoretical and practical concepts were taught to us. The first semester was full of fundamental ideas like Data Structures, Algorithms, Human Computer Interaction, Database Management, and other concepts. I thought, this is beginning to look like CS. I was excited, given that these are more advanced concepts, and exactly what I signed up for.

Reality Hits

Except, now in the second semester, I was surprised that most of our subjects expected mastery of the concepts AND MORE. We had our software engineering 1 subject this semester, and surprisingly, we were on our own with the development of our own software. It wasn't that the professor gave us freedom, it was that we expected guidance and lessons on more languages that we could use in our development process. We had to self-learn a lot of concepts to plan and develop our output, which was terrifying. It made me question if this would be our situation for the rest of our journey. What do you mean I have to learn a lot more by myself on top of what is provided by our professor? Other subjects also expect us to choose our own language and our own way of thinking and solving things, which I guess is an important skill to have as a programmer and a computer science major, but still, it's baffling to experience first hand.

What Lies Ahead

As the journey continues, they advised us that it's only going to get WORSE (dun dun dunnnn). With the upcoming thesis and the internships, I don't really see how my current skillset is enough. That's the situation of a lot of other students, because I get that this degree does require a lot of self-learning and we're not supposed to just end our learning in the classroom, but it's still a terrifying thought nonetheless.

Facing It

I can't say to myself that this is lesson-learned, because even when faced with these experiences that force you out of your comfort zone, I still am not exactly doing my part in self-learning things for my own sake. I barely know how to use git and GitHub, which became a crucial part of my studies all of a sudden, so I was forced to self-learn it. I really thought professors would teach us git, but they never got the chance to because we already had to use it. I would love to think of that as a small win somehow, being able to at least know how to type git commit -m on my terminal and not have to Google what it does every time.

Realizations

I think that unpreparedness and relying too much on textbook knowledge is the first and most common reason why we, tech students, have career anxiety. On my journey, those moments where they expected too much when I had too little was more than enough to make me realize that I had to fend for myself and step out of that box I made for myself. I can't exactly boast to anyone that I realized that, because I haven't exactly done much about it, but hey, at least you know that I'm just like you, and we're in this together. Knowing what you need to improve is the first step to improvement, and this journey might be scary and only getting worse as we move forward, but we'll get there eventually, just push through and persevere. We're all still figuring things out as we go, and for now, that's enough.

Advice

  1. Step out of your comfort zone and embrace self-learning.
  2. Recognize areas where you're unprepared and work to improve them.
  3. Accept that some guidance won't always be given; take initiative.
  4. Understand that identifying what you need to improve is a key first step.
  5. Push through challenges even if it feels scary; perseverance matters.
