Drowning in Tutorials, Starving for Skills

OBILLO, MON ANDREI March 20, 2026 5 min read

There comes a point where we stop thinking and start doing, and at this point, we all get it. There’s a lot of reasons to be anxious about our future, but that anxiousness never really goes away on its own. I’ve realized throughout my journey that I barely move from where I am if I don’t act and don’t do what I must to learn and continue forward. I understand that someday we’ll have to face the future we’re often dreading about, but the least we can do for ourselves is to prepare and make sure we’re actually ready for it.

The Flood of Resources

Now, there’s millions of resources out there that teach us concepts and help us kickstart our journey. I mean, it’s the benefit of being in today’s times, where information is one click away. I’ve used this advantage so many times, whether it was to understand how to solve something in calculus, or to find solutions to a coding problem, or even to learn a new algorithm. It’s truly been a huge part of my journey so far. It has often made me feel like I’m actually getting somewhere. But, once I actually sit down and face problems that aren’t as obvious, it all crumbles down.

Knowledge Without Application

It seems so far, the overconsumption of online tutorials and resources has left me with a bunch of basic knowledge and no real-world application to it. What am I supposed to do with a queue except make a ticket line simulation, right? I do feel a bit lost each time I start something, and that’s just school work. What about in the real world? Not actually knowing how to use those knowledge in practical situations can be just as scary as all other reasons I’ve reflected upon for career anxiety. We try so hard to learn so much, and yet when faced with something actually real, we’ve got nothing.

Learning Through Struggle

One thing a professor has said to me about this was that real understanding begins when we struggle. When we face problems and have a hard time solving them, that’s when we actually learn. No amount of textbook knowledge can teach us what facing problems head-on can. And yes, I can actually attest to that. One particular experience I had was in my software engineering class. We’ve got so much textbook knowledge on coding, concepts such as HCI, OOP, etc., and yet we learned so much more when our professor actually put us in a situation where we had to figure things out instead of being spoon-fed information. I mean, as the system designer in our project, it literally pushed me to learn so many concepts on digital art just to keep up with the workload for me. I was pushed to the limit a lot of times, and I think my past self from first year would’ve quit within the first few hours of starting on our project. I wasn’t at all perfect, and I still do make mistakes, but look at where I am now with that. I grew so much.

Progress in Small Steps

And to think, I barely noticed that I improved until I looked back, now that the semester is almost over. It was incremental, like small baby steps, and looking back, those steps did add up. People who observe from the outside might look at my progress and say that I improved so quickly, but I wasn’t able to see that. It wasn’t overnight.

Keep Moving Forward

And you know, I still might not be there yet, and I’ve got a lot more to improve, but maybe, in a few months, I can’t say the same thing about myself. Maybe in a few months, I got what it takes. What I achieved now was never in our modules, nor was it in any textbooks. It was with something harder, but more rewarding. And you know what, I never could’ve reached this far if I didn’t move inch by inch, no matter how hard it got. Even small steps matter, you know. And if you’re doing something, that’s enough.

Advice

  1. Don’t rely solely on tutorials; practical experience is crucial.
  2. Real understanding comes from struggling and solving real problems.
  3. Actively apply knowledge instead of passively consuming content.
  4. Move from thinking to doing; preparation alone isn’t enough.
  5. Focus on building practical skills, not just theoretical knowledge.
