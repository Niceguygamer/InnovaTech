Discipline: The Hard Part No One Likes

OBILLO, MON ANDREI March 20, 2026 5 min read

We’ve established the need to move forward, no matter how hard things become, but sometimes, that pushes us over the limit. We’ve talked about ragequitting on goals because we burnout and lose passion, and we’ve talked about how rest is important. But, we must also face the fact that in the real world, we can’t always put that first. Consistency and discipline is an important quality to have in being in the tech industry.

Burnout is Normal

We’ve got a very long way ahead, and we can’t exactly just expect to navigate through that if we burnout too fast, right? That’s partially right. The thing is, burn out is normal, and unavoidable, especially because a lot of us encounter a lot of things for the first time. We’re all still students, and we’re so new to this that we don’t exactly have all the answers at once. It can be quite tiring to continue the path towards your career, considering the amount of work you need to put in to learn everything, and the pressure of having a career path. We often get so clouded by our emotions.

The Importance of Discipline and Consistency

I get that all previous entries I have made all point towards relatability and how each one of us experience the same things. But, I must also push you towards a more disciplined mindset. Let’s face it, in the real world, no one would actually care if we don’t have passion anymore. I’ve talked about the consequences of real world situations, and that it’s so unforgiving. I am telling you, it is actually unforgiving. Discipline and consistency is one of the most reliable ways of making sure you don’t mess up in your career.

How to Develop Discipline

But how do I even develop discipline and consistency? A good friend of mine, also our Team Leader in this project, has told me quite a few of these tips to actually develop discipline and be consistent. First was that you need to prioritize progress over perfection, because we can’t expect to progress if we stick for too long in one thing. I actually experienced burnout while trying to perfect something in the past, and it prevented me from continuing at the same pace. Another thing was that rest was important. Even in that disciplined mindset, it’s important to actually find time to rest, as it’s a key part of being consistent. Consistency comes from a well-rested mind and body, and you can’t expect consistency if you push yourself too hard.

Realistic Goal Setting

One thing our team leader has often excelled in doing is to make sure the deadlines work for all of us. Realistic goal setting is a huge deal for developing discipline. Breaking apart a task into manageable pieces helps to make sure you don’t quit midway through, thus helping you learn discipline. Sustainable efforts matter the most in discipline.

Consistency Over Perfection

Consistency isn’t about doing everything perfectly or working endlessly without pause. It’s about showing up every day, putting in the effort that’s sustainable, and learning to balance work with rest. Discipline is built gradually, through repeated actions, reflection, and adjustments to what’s working and what’s not.

Pacing Yourself in the Tech Industry

The tech industry will always test you with deadlines, complex problems, and high expectations. But if you can learn to pace yourself, focus on progress, and take care of your mind and body, you’ll be able to navigate the challenges without losing yourself to burnout.

Final Thoughts

So, remember this: it’s okay if you don’t get it right the first time. It’s okay if you pause to rest or recalibrate. What matters is that you keep going, consistently and sustainably. Every small step counts, and every small win adds up. Steady effort, not exhaustion, is enough.

Advice

  1. Prioritize progress over perfection.
  2. Take rest seriously; a well-rested mind and body enable consistency.
  3. Set realistic goals and break tasks into manageable pieces.
  4. Focus on sustainable effort rather than overworking yourself.
  5. Show up consistently, even if imperfectly.
  6. Reflect on what’s working and adjust your approach as needed.
  7. Pace yourself to avoid burnout; small, steady steps matter.
  8. Balance work with rest; discipline isn’t about nonstop effort.
