Why We Ragequit (It's Not Just Games)

OBILLO, MON ANDREI March 20, 2026 5 min read

I’ve ragequitted from a lot of things more than once in my life. Not just in games, although the majority of my ragequits are in games, but also in a lot of goals I set in my life. It often starts off strong, with that thrill of trying out new things, whether I’ll write a new fictional work, start a new hobby, or even start a new personal project. Then, it turns to frustration. I stare blankly into an empty page, or I accidentally miss a day of doing my hobby, or I scroll endlessly through google and try to reason out with AI, but in the end, I just quit. That passion burns out too quickly, and I’m left with nothing.

Where It Started

I once had a web development class in junior high school, and back then, HTML5 wasn’t yet available (just to give context). I thought to myself, I really enjoyed the class, and I was able to do a lot more than what was taught to us, mainly because I hated the design choices of my teacher then, but also because my interest was piqued. I spent a lot of time searching for CSS snippets I can use to achieve my desired look. Then, I became determined to make my own HTML/CSS project for myself, just a simple About Me website, and one that I’ll just happily keep to myself. And so I started to plan it. I wanted a sidebar, and it was going to be very modern-looking, and I was going to add several elements that I wanted. All that planning, and then… nothing. I started to type my code, and then… nothing. I quickly got bored and just quit just before I started to code it. It had everything ready, except I lost that desire and passion to make my own website the way I wanted it to be.

Another Attempt

Now in college, I was thinking about possible simple projects I can do for myself, and I thought, well, I need a way to track my allowance spending using the system that I use to compute it (by system, I mean the way I do things, because I’m in some sort of postpaid way of getting my allowance). I was given my daily allowance, and each month, I had to pay back my mom the amount she had given me, because I have a monthly stipend that I get from my scholarship. I had all my needed variables, the ones I use to compute it, and just need a way to automatically log it so I don’t have to manually check it at the end of the month. Sounds simple enough, right? And so I decided the easiest way was to make it web-based. But, low and behold, I encountered my first problem. I didn’t know how to record it without a database. And so I searched it and found out about Local Storage. A simple solution, so that’s that. What about deployment? I asked ChatGPT, and it recommended that I just use an HTML file and pin it to my home screen for easy access. And so now, all I had to do was code. And… nothing. I got so busy that I forgot for a while, and when I remembered it, I had lost that desire to even finish it. I just resorted back to computing on my own.

More Things I Dropped

I have tried several other projects, like making my own simple mobile app using Java, which I decided was too hard to even comprehend, and starting my journey towards understanding machine learning and deep learning. All that was in vain. It wasn’t just the projects, it was also the other goals I had that I abandoned. I would love to give excuses, but it all boils down to the fact that I lost that desire to continue.

Why It Happens

Maybe its because of too much planning, or being distracted, or making excuses, or even because of not having the right skills. It’s true, passion burns out if it’s left burning without fuel for too long. Looking back, I took too long to plan out things, even making diagrams and writing pages long documentation before even starting out code. I got too caught up in thinking about what makes my project amazing that I eventually ran out of fuel to even start. For some projects, like learning ML and DL, I truly wasn’t passionate about it nor do I actually want to pursue it. I just figured I wanted to hop on to that trend of learning AI and applying machine learning concepts. Maybe it’s also because there’s not enough passion to begin with.

What I Realized

In the vast field of computer science, I think the worst thing you can do is waste your time with projects that don’t matter to YOU. Even in general, the worst thing you can do is waste time. If you want to start something, then actually start. You can plan, but don’t consume your time and energy on planning. Things can work out eventually, and you can figure it out as you go along. If you want to actually achieve something, then actually do something. It matters more if you made something than if you planned something perfect, because the result is what others see. It’s also a huge reward for yourself, having small, or even big wins.

Moving Forward

Even in your future career, you can’t expect to succeed in it if you don’t love it. First step is always to make sure you love what you do. Then, don’t waste time and energy thinking about doing something instead of actually doing it, rather allow imperfections. And ragequitting is inevitable, and it gives you what you need to know. If you ragequit on something, it might be because it’s not for you, and that’s a huge step towards your goals.

Final Thought

We can always start and figure it out along the way, and that’s enough.

Advice

  1. Don’t let passion burn out by overplanning; start doing.
  2. Focus on projects that truly matter to you.
  3. Small wins matter and provide motivation to continue.
  4. Embrace imperfection; results matter more than perfect planning.
  5. Ragequitting can be a sign that something isn’t for you, which helps clarify goals.
