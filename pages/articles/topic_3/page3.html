The Side Project That Taught Me More Than Any Class

LEONGSUN, EUWIN March 31, 2026 5 min read

During senior high school I started building a personal project mostly out of curiosity. I had no roadmap, no mentor, and honestly no idea what I was doing half the time. That project ended up teaching me more about real development than any single subject I took in college.

The project was called Prior. The idea started simple enough: I wanted to build something I could actually use. Over time it grew into a full platform with multiple services connected together. A social network, cloud storage, an AI chatbot, a messaging system. I was figuring it out as I went, one broken feature at a time.

I am not sharing this to talk about the project. I am sharing it because the process of building it was genuinely the most effective learning I have done. Not because it was always fun. Because it was real. The problems were real. The bugs were real. The frustration of something not working for hours and then finally clicking was real.

Why Building Something Beats Just Studying

When you are working through a textbook or a tutorial, there is always a safety net. The answer is somewhere nearby. The example works because someone designed it to work. Real projects do not give you that. When something breaks and you have no idea why, you have to actually figure it out.

That pressure is exactly what forces you to learn. You cannot move forward until you understand the problem. You cannot skip the chapter. You read the documentation, look for similar issues, try things, and eventually something either works or teaches you why it does not. That cycle is where real skill development happens.

Every time I got stuck building Prior and worked through it, I understood that concept on a level no lecture could replicate. Databases, authentication, routing, APIs, deployment, error handling. I learned all of it because I needed to, not because it was on the syllabus.

How to Start When You Do Not Feel Ready

The most common reason people put off building their own project is that they feel like they are not ready yet. They want to finish one more course, get a bit better at the language, understand a few more concepts first. I had the same feeling when I started. I still have it sometimes.

That feeling does not go away. There will always be something you do not know yet. Waiting until you feel ready is just another way of not starting. The readiness comes from doing the work, not from preparing to do it.

Start with something small. Something you would actually want to use yourself. It does not have to be impressive or original. A simple tool that solves a small problem you have. The goal is not to build something perfect. The goal is to run into problems you do not know how to solve yet and work through them.

You will Google everything. That is fine. Looking things up is not cheating, it is just how development works. What matters is that you understand what you find and can apply it.
