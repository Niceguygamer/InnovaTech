Nobody Warned Me About the Skills Gap

March 31, 2026 5 min read

For most people, skills development in tech starts in college out of necessity. For me it started earlier, in senior high, and not because I had to. It started because I wanted to build something real.

I was not pushed into it by a deadline or a grade. Honestly, at that point most of my batchmates were just following the school syllabus, doing what was required, and that was fine. But I had this pull toward actually making something. Not a school project. Something that worked, something other people could use, something I could put out into the world and call mine.

Part of it came from people I looked up to. Seeing what builders like Elon Musk had created, reading about founders who started with nothing but an idea and a laptop, made me think about what I could actually do with the skills I was picking up. It was not about copying anyone. It was about that feeling of wanting to push yourself to create something that goes beyond the classroom.

That combination of inspiration, genuine passion, and the specific desire to build a platform that others could actually use is what got me started. And it is what I think separates people who develop real competence early from those who only pick it up once they are forced to.

What Competence Actually Means

When people say someone is competent in tech, they do not just mean that person can pass a written exam. They mean that person can sit down, look at a problem, and figure out how to solve it. They can build something from scratch. They can debug something that is broken. They can explain what their code does to someone else. That is a different skill set from what most college subjects test you on.

It is not about being naturally gifted at this. It is about having done enough real work that when a problem shows up, you have some idea of where to start. That only comes from practice. And the people who get that practice early are usually the ones who were chasing something they actually cared about, not just a grade.

Why This Matters for You

You do not need to have started in senior high. That is not the point. The point is that the drive has to come from somewhere real. A problem you want to solve, something you want to build, a person you want to prove something to, even just yourself. Competence is a side effect of caring enough to keep going when things get hard.

The gap between knowing theory and being able to actually do the work is real. Most people hit it at some point in their tech program. But the ones who close it fastest are the ones who have a reason to. The rest of this series is about how to build that competence, one practical step at a time.
