I Believe In You: Choose Alignment with Work Ethic & Vision, Not Competence

ALBARICO, IZYLLE ANNE March 31, 2026 5 min read

Skills can be learned, but values cannot. Reliable, vision-aligned teammates create stronger teams than competence alone.

Skills Can Be Learned, Values Cannot The most important realization I’ve had in my time leading is that you should choose members not because of competence but because of compatibility in work ethic and alignment of vision and goals. The traits you should look for are whether they value timeliness, innovation, ambition, accountability, and responsibility—not just whether they are excellent. Looking back at all the teams I managed, I realized that the ones with less stress and conflict were not the most skilled or intelligent, but the ones willing to listen, follow, and do their best. They don’t need to be the most skilled; they just need to be reliable and willing to learn. I would even say that assigning the most responsibility to those who were reliable and responsible, regardless of skill level, was one of the best decisions I ever made.

Skills but Different Work Ethic vs. Unskilled but Reliable

I have worked with two different teams: one I considered a dream team composed of top performers in the class, and another that was a regular team. You might think the dream team would be easy to manage, but you’d be wrong. Many of the members thought they knew better and refused to follow the instructions or structures I created. They insisted on doing things their own way, creating a world separate from the team. It felt more like individuals in a group rather than a unified team. In contrast, the regular team was more willing to listen, comply with direction, and act in relation to one another rather than as individuals. I assigned heavy tasks to members who were reliable and trustworthy, even if they weren’t the most skilled, and it was the best decision I made.

From this experience, I learned that what makes an excellent team is not the best members but the most compatible ones—those who share the same values, standards, and vision. It is better to assign work to someone reliable than to someone who thinks they know better and resists alignment. Competence alone does not equal excellent teamwork.

Competence Is Not Equal to Excellent Teamwork

If you have the ability to choose your team members, don’t choose based on friendship or perceived competence. Choose people who share your work ethic and vision. Skills can be learned, competence can be improved, but attitude and values are much harder to change. It takes a lot of effort and time to shift someone’s mindset, and you don’t always have that luxury. If you can’t choose your members, then promote open communication, practice active listening, give trust and autonomy, cultivate collaboration, and set clear expectations and goals so that your team has a clear and singular direction.

5 Tips for Choosing Team Members

  1. Look for alignment in work ethic.
  2. Look for reliability and accountability.
  3. Look for willingness to learn.
  4. Look for openness to take risks and desire for innovation.
  5. Look for alignment with vision, standards, and goals.
