I Hear You: The Art of Active Listening

March 31, 2026 5 min read

Listening is about aligning ideas, not rejecting them. Respectful dialogue fosters creativity, trust, and collective ownership.

I want to make my team members feel heard, for I have experienced the same. This is the goal I want to achieve for my personal growth as a leader and for the experience I want to give my team. Listening is part of our senses—it is basically second nature. But why is it difficult to do?

Active Listening vs Passive Listening

Personally, I tend to filter irrelevant information or details. I have a hard time simply listening without assessing if it is feasible and whether it aligns with the overall goal, which makes me quite dismissive—shutting down my team members unintentionally. An example of this was during a brainstorming session for our group project. One member suggested a vampire theme even though the concept had already been decided as “sand.” Even though it was just the initial concept, I visibly rejected the idea by suggesting that the team should think of something that aligned directly with the existing theme. This was not just one instance; it happened multiple times. Because of that, a member I relied on for creative ideas became silent during meetings and no longer spoke up or shared anything.

I realized this also affected how the rest of the team behaved. Since they saw this happen to another member, there was a high chance they assumed it would happen to them too. My tendency was hurting the team, the members, and their trust in me more than anything. I had to change this shortcoming immediately.

Align Ideas, Don’t Reject

Reflecting, I realized that I shouldn’t shut down ideas and reject them immediately without giving them a chance beyond my own thinking. I recall the time when I was not a team leader but a member in senior high school. I was under a leadership where I felt heard and valued, and my ideas actually mattered. Because of that, I felt more motivated to do my best since it was my idea coming to life. I want this same feeling and experience for my team because I know how it feels and how the team benefits more than anything.

On the other hand, I also know how it feels when my ideas are rejected—outright dismissed the exact moment I shared them. I was almost in tears but held it in, not only because I was unfairly dismissed but because the leader raised their voice unnecessarily, which made the rest of the team silent. To tell you the truth, that person was not a good example of true leadership but rather what a bully looks like. Fortunately, I am not like that and won’t let myself become that. I ensure I uphold the utmost respect and professionalism even if I disagree.

Active Listening

Active listening is about listening openly without pre-judgment but with an open mind. It means choosing to pause and consider an idea and how to make it align with the existing structure and goals instead of outright rejecting or dismissing it. Active listening makes people feel seen, valued, and motivated to do more and produce quality output because they feel involved—not just followers. That is what I want to happen. I want to make my team members feel heard, for I have experienced the same.

5 Tips for Mastering the Art of Active Listening

  1. Only listen and focus—don’t think of anything else.
  2. Listen attentively, absorbing what’s being said; stop the urge to interrupt.
  3. If not feasible or misaligned, don’t reject; instead, discuss with your team how to align it.
  4. After discussion, if it is still not feasible or aligned, reject it constructively—acknowledge the idea to show that it mattered but explain why it won’t be accepted.
  5. Uphold the utmost professionalism and respect at all times.
