Prefer Noise than Silence: Ways to Build and Foster Open Communication

ALBARICO, IZYLLE ANNE March 31, 2026 5 min read

Silence breeds confusion; open communication, even if messy, ensures clarity, prevents resentment, and strengthens team cohesion.

Guys, if you have any concerns or clarifications, just let me know. This is the phrase I often use and tell my team after making announcements about instructions and deadlines. As a leader, communication is a vital tool for progress and decisions. If it is not present, it causes delay and misalignment in the directions of the project output, which basically causes a breakdown in the team’s cohesion.

Self-Blame, Normalcy vs Communication Style & Respect

My personal experiences with conflict in communication and what I learned from it are with my team that I will call “Lesson.” Lesson is a team composed of excellent students, the best of the best from the entire class. However, that doesn’t mean managing this group was easy. I would even consider this team my hardest challenge yet because despite their excellence, there were two people in this group that I had a hard time reaching and communicating with compared to the rest. They didn’t reply nor acknowledge messages. If they did, it was last minute. Their answers were short and lacked details that would prompt you to follow up or chase them for more information. It was tremendously frustrating trying to engage with them.

At first, I thought I was the problem or that this was normal, but when I compared this to the rest of the members who responded well and my other teams that I was also handling, I realized that this was not normal nor was I the problem. It was due to the difference in communication style and the lack of respect. Even if there is indeed a difference, that doesn’t excuse the silence and the lack of acknowledgement when I have made it clear that communication is important because it affects the team overall.

Policies and Adjustments

A meeting was scheduled and held to address the communication issue. We discussed the reasons for the delays, the silence, the lack of acknowledgement, and the lack of details—basically everything that needed to be addressed. I learned that the two members who I had issues with preferred much more concise announcements and messages. They also didn’t like replying and would rather react than reply, but they said that if it was a question, they would reply and answer with sufficient details to avoid follow-ups. Once these policies were created and applied, a visible and positive change happened. Communication improved and was better compared to before.

Open Communication

It is about adjusting each communication style to meet halfway while being respectful, and creating a safe environment where help is guaranteed—not just a word without meaning. Learning how to accept “no,” and not negotiating to force something to happen but compromising to ensure that all needs and limitations are considered and met. So when I ask my team, “Guys, if you have any concerns or clarifications, just let me know,” I sincerely mean it.

5 Tips for Fostering Effective Open Communication

  1. Know your team members’ communication style.
  2. Adjust towards each other’s preferences and uphold respect at all times.
  3. Enforce policies agreed upon and adjust regularly to ensure effectiveness.
  4. Accept “no” for an answer, don’t negotiate but compromise.
  5. Follow through on your words—if you tell them you will help, then help.
