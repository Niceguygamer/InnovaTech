Below the Belt: Distinguishing Constructive Criticism from Attacks

March 31, 2026 5 min read

Constructive feedback targets actions, not identity. Personal attacks disguised as feedback must be called out and stopped.

You shouldn’t feel insulted or targeted—that’s not feedback, that’s an attack. Feedback should not describe you personally but focus on your actions, behavior, or the systems in place. These are things that can be solved productively and that heavily affect team performance, not an individual’s identity or preferences. Honest feedback should not be sharp bluntness that disregards the receiver’s emotions or feelings. Nothing productive results from that—only defensiveness and arguments lacking professionalism and sensitivity. Encourage your team to give honest feedback, but not in a way that is offensive or directed at someone’s identity. Feedback should remain polite and solution-oriented. Instead of just sharing the problem, suggest solutions on how to resolve it.

Focus on the Solution, Not the Person

I had a team member I would describe as blunt and insensitive. He gave feedback in an accusatory way, saying I didn’t care about the other team members or that I only cared about myself and deadlines. At some point, I even doubted myself. I do put the team under immense pressure because I want to prevent us from failing to submit on time. That experience left me feeling helpless and traumatized. I couldn’t let my team be under the mercy of someone like that.

Maybe they were right—I indeed didn’t care. Because of that, I spiraled for two days, unable to sleep, shaking my mind on how I could show that I cared for their well-being. I thought of creating a survey form asking about their mental health, stress levels, capacity to work, feedback on my leadership, and suggestions to improve their experience. After doing that, I recalled the times I felt guilty for making my team work so hard that I would tear up and cry frequently each time I tightened my grip to ensure we completed tasks on time. The reason I was doing this was to prevent hopelessness. I created a survey that no one even bothered to answer, but it was there waiting for them. I laid out and organized everything they needed to complete the task. Would you call that not caring and selfish?

I now realize that what I received was not constructive criticism but a personal attack disguised as feedback. If that person truly cared about how I felt and appreciated the responsibilities I carried, they wouldn’t have said those things. At one point, they said my deadlines were useless. That did not describe the problem or solve anything—it was an attack directed at me personally, not the issue.

Identify Constructive Criticism

It is important to know when feedback is helpful and when it is not, to protect your sanity and truly solve problems. Feedback is an observation or commentary used to improve the system. The focus should be on what the system can do, not on personal identity. When feedback becomes personal and the receiver feels the urge to defend themselves or question their identity, that’s an attack. It should be called out and stopped. Professionalism must be upheld at all times, regardless of personal feelings toward the individual.

5 Tips to Identify and Handle Attacks

  1. If you feel it was more about you than the problem, call it out.
  2. Ask for it to stop, then pivot to something more productive.
  3. If you start doubting yourself, remind yourself of what you actually did or didn’t do.
  4. If it is constructive feedback, listen calmly—don’t defend.
  5. Uphold the utmost level of professionalism at all times.
