I Won’t Suffocate You: Building Trust & Giving Autonomy

ALBARICO, IZYLLE ANNE March 31, 2026 5 min read

Control suffocates; trust empowers. Giving autonomy builds morale, patience, and efficiency, while reducing pressure.

Be tolerant with others and strict with yourself. – Marcus Aurelius. The meaning of this quote is about not controlling others but yourself. To tell you the truth, this part is where I struggle the most—letting go of control. It’s not because I’m a control freak; I believe I am not. It’s because I expect highly of others, and when they don’t match my expectations, I firm up and start managing more tightly. This is bad because it pressures and suffocates my team. They feel they are being watched every step of the way, and I don’t want that. This has to change. Your team needs trust more than anything. They don’t need control; they need guidance.

Don’t Assume Non-Compliance, Act Accordingly After Waiting

I have a story where my tendency to add pressure or control when I don’t see progress or when met with silence resulted in decreased morale and poor performance. I was managing a team I called the “Cats” Team. This team was composed of independent thinkers who preferred loose structure more than anything, especially one particular member I’ll call “Type B.” This member kept advocating for looser deadlines, arguing that we shouldn’t set deadlines too early because it sacrifices the quality of the output and puts too much pressure on the team. They even warned me that if I didn’t change how I managed my team, it would collapse.

This wasn’t the only time I received such feedback. I also heard it in senior high school and with another team in college. I started questioning myself and developing a fear that if I didn’t change, people would either stop following me altogether or wouldn’t want to work with me again. This made me reflect on why I had this tendency and how to prevent it. Looking back, I realized I placed my team members on the same pedestal I placed myself on—my own standard for quality, timeliness, and structure. I automatically assumed people had the same values. So when they didn’t show it, I thought they were disrespecting me, instead of recognizing that some people don’t reply immediately but that doesn’t mean they won’t comply. Some people couldn’t meet deadlines because something unexpected happened, but they couldn’t notify me immediately. That doesn’t mean they weren’t trying to comply on time.

People have different lives than ours. We can’t say, “If I can do it, why can’t they?” The answer is simply because they are not us and should not be compared to us. Instead, they should be met with empathy, patience, and trust.

Trust and Autonomy

Trust is something hard to give, especially when you are used to being let down or when the stakes are high. But honestly speaking, giving trust is more beneficial than withholding it. With trust, your team will feel comfortable informing you of delays as soon as they arise. This helps address problems earlier rather than last minute, which sacrifices the quality of the output. It also keeps morale high. Your team will feel motivated and work more efficiently if they are not constantly monitored or distracted by frequent pings. Trust leads to a more effective and motivated team.

5 Tips to Build Trust and Cultivate Autonomy

  1. Have your default mindset set to: they will do it and they can be trusted, unless proven wrong.
  2. If they can’t comply on time, help instead of punishing them. The goal is to complete on time, not to punish. Always have buffer days to account for delays.
  3. Don’t set the same standard you set for yourself because you will only be disappointed. See them as they are, not what you want them to be.
  4. Give them time to complete their task without constantly hovering or monitoring them. Only set one reminder on the same day it is due, not in advance.
  5. Practice mindfulness by pausing and showing empathy for limitations. If delays are too frequent and without accountability, take action to address and intervene.
