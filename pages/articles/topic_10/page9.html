Let’s Talk: Learning to Be Comfortable with Tension or Disharmony

ALBARICO, IZYLLE ANNE March 31, 2026 5 min read

Conflict is not a threat but a tool for growth. Addressing tension calmly strengthens trust, autonomy, and team alignment.

In my experience as a leader, I never saw tension or friction as something to be avoided. I consider it a way for the team to improve and grow. By acknowledging and addressing friction, you resolve issues before they get out of hand and resentment builds up. This is the same with asking the team to make revisions. When I see that the output seems rushed or lacks quality, I ask them to revise it. When certain behaviors delay decision-making or progress, I call them out to change. Everything that affects the team, I confront, address, and resolve calmly and professionally.

Addressing It, Don’t Ignore It

I have had my fair share of conflicts and friction with different teams I’ve handled. Each time, we came out stronger and better. One team I call “Eye Opener” constantly proved my past assumptions wrong. At first, I thought they would have problems with deadlines because during one task, they started completing their work at the last minute, and some members submitted incorrect outputs. Before proceeding with project development, I made it clear in a meeting that deadlines must be complied with as much as possible. If they were unsure of something, they should ask before proceeding, otherwise they would waste time and risk compromising quality due to limited time.

Initially, I thought nothing would change, based on past experiences. But I was wrong. Members began apologizing and notifying me in advance if there would be delays. I was shocked and pleasantly surprised. It made me realize those talks truly mattered and led to changes. I no longer tightened control but instead gave them more autonomy and trust.

Disharmony

I have worked with different people in teams, including peacekeepers—those who go with the flow of the majority and leave decision-making to others to conserve energy. Over time, I saw them improve and become more assertive, which I was glad to witness. It was character development from passive to assertive. I value assertiveness because it allows people to say what needs to be said and done, rather than wasting time hesitating. Instead of spending time deliberating, they could have spent it acting. Be comfortable with friction or tension, especially when you want to achieve a goal. Sometimes you need to be the “bad guy” so that in the end, the team’s effort is not wasted and goals are achieved.

5 Tips to Be Comfortable with Disharmony

  1. Treat it like rewiring—it is important for your team to work smoothly.
  2. Deliver feedback professionally and calmly to reduce unnecessary drama.
  3. Be clear and direct about what you want to address.
  4. Focus on solving the problem constructively and productively.
  5. If emotions rise, give the team time to collect themselves before proceeding.
