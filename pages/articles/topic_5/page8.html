Topic # 8

Note-Taking for People Who Learn by Doing

March 20, 2026 5 min read

First year, notes were taken at every single lecture. Thick, detailed, color-organized notebooks that looked incredibly thorough. They were almost never opened again. Not because the material did not matter — but because in a practical tech course, the note is not the learning. The doing is the learning. And if your note-taking strategy was built for a literature class, it is probably failing you in your programming courses.

Reading a written description of how a loop works does not build the same understanding as actually writing fifty loops across different problems. In tech programs, there is a meaningful difference between two kinds of notes worth keeping. Concept notes capture what something is, why it exists, and what problem it solves — these are worth writing carefully and reviewing regularly. Process notes capture how something works in practice, the syntax, the common errors, what breaks — these are better kept as personal documentation than as traditional written notes. A GitHub repository of annotated code you wrote yourself, organized by topic, is often more useful for review than any notebook.

The Teach-Back Method

The highest-leverage note-taking habit for tech students is deceptively simple. After a lecture, close everything and write a brief explanation of what you just learned as if you are explaining it to someone who has never seen it before. No jargon. No copying from slides. Pure reconstruction from your own understanding. The gaps that appear in your explanation are exactly the gaps in your actual knowledge — and identifying them immediately after a lecture is infinitely more efficient than discovering them the night before an exam.

Good notes for a tech student are not a transcript of what was said. They are a map of what was understood. And the process of building that map — struggling to reconstruct, identifying gaps, filling them in — is where a significant part of the actual learning happens.

Tips to Remember

  • After every lecture, write a teach-back summary before reopening your notes — identify your gaps while the material is still fresh
  • Keep an annotated code repository alongside your written notes, with comments explaining what each piece does and why
  • When you solve a hard problem or fix a tricky bug, document the process — that becomes one of your most valuable study resources
  • Write less during lectures and process more — understanding notes beat transcription notes every time
  • Review notes within 24 hours, not the night before the exam — the forgetting happens faster than you think
