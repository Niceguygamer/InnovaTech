The Routine That Actually Runs

AGUNOD, IÑIGO JOSE March 20, 2026 5 min read

Every Sunday, a perfectly color-coded weekly schedule. Every Tuesday, it is already falling apart — a debugging session ran three hours over, a groupmate bailed, a new assignment dropped out of nowhere. By Thursday, the plan is completely abandoned and panic studying takes over. Sound familiar? If you are in a tech program, it probably does. The problem was never the schedule. The problem was building something rigid for a workload that is fundamentally unpredictable.

Tech programs break normal schedules because the work is variable in a way most other courses are not. One assignment can take 45 minutes or six hours depending on how many bugs you hit. A concept can click in an afternoon or take three days to sink in. Rigid time-blocking creates the illusion of control and then shatters the moment reality does not cooperate. The students who stay consistent are not the ones with the most detailed planners — they are the ones who built flexibility into their system from the start.

An anchor-Based Routine

What works better than a rigid schedule is an anchor-based routine — two or three fixed daily blocks where studying happens no matter what. Not tied to a specific subject, just committed time. Within those blocks, you adapt to what the week actually demands. Think of it like your program's main function. The structure stays consistent. The logic inside adapts to whatever input the week throws at it.

Consistency in when you show up matters far more than rigidity in what you do when you get there. A flexible routine you actually follow will always beat a perfect schedule you abandon by Wednesday.

Tips to Remember

  • Pick two fixed daily study blocks and treat them as non-negotiable, even if they are only 30 minutes
  • At the start of each week, identify your three most important tasks and make sure those happen first
  • Build buffer time specifically for debugging — tech work always takes longer than planned
  • Keep a running task list instead of a rigid hourly plan so you can stay flexible
  • Do a brief Sunday reset — not to judge the past week, just to adjust and set direction for the next one
