Your Brain Is Not a Hard Drive

AGUNOD, IÑIGO JOSE March 20, 2026 5 min read

First year of a tech program, most students try to memorize their way through the semester. Every function, every command, color-coded and flashcarded. Then the practical exam arrives and the flashcards are completely useless. If you are studying IT, computer science, or any tech-related course, this is the moment you realize that memorizing syntax and actually understanding code are two completely different things.

Your brain is not built like a database. It does not retrieve information cleanly on command the way a computer pulls a file from storage. What it does exceptionally well is make connections — linking new concepts to things it already understands, building mental frameworks that let you reason through unfamiliar problems. You will never memorize every method in a library or every rule in a framework. But if you understand the underlying logic of how something works, you can figure out almost anything even when you forget the exact syntax.

The "Why" Before the "How"

One habit that separates students who genuinely excel from those who just scrape by is always asking why before how. Why does this algorithm exist? Why is the database structured this way? Why does this approach solve the problem better than the alternative? That context transforms isolated pieces of information into a mental model — and mental models are what keep you functional when things break, which in tech, they always eventually do.

In a field that changes as fast as technology does, the ability to think and reason will always outlast the ability to memorize. Build understanding first, and the details will follow far more naturally than any flashcard session could ever produce.

Tips to Remember

  • Before learning how something works, understand what problem it was built to solve
  • After a lecture, close everything and explain the concept out loud in plain language — if you cannot, you do not understand it yet
  • Practice on problems you have never seen before, not just the examples from your notes
  • When you get something wrong, find the gap in understanding — do not just memorize the correct answer
  • Learn to read official documentation — it is one of the most practical skills you will build in this program
