Topic # 4

Deep Work in a World Full of Notifications

March 20, 2026 5 min read

At any given study session, the average tech student has fifteen to forty browser tabs open. The lecture recording, the Stack Overflow thread, the docs page, the group chat, the YouTube video that was supposed to be a five-minute break two hours ago. This is not a laziness problem. It is what happens when your study environment and your distraction environment are the exact same place — and nobody ever taught you how to separate them.

The hard truth is that you cannot learn recursion while half-watching a video. You cannot trace an algorithm while your notifications are going off. The kind of thinking that tech programs demand — logical, layered, and precise — requires long uninterrupted stretches of concentration that most students never actually give themselves. And the longer you go without practicing that kind of focus, the harder it becomes to access it when you actually need it, like during an exam or a live debugging session.

Focus is a Condition you Build

The fix is not willpower — willpower runs out by 2pm. The fix is designing your environment so that distraction is harder than focusing. Website blockers during study sessions. Phone in another room, not just flipped over. A space your brain associates with work, not rest. One clear goal written down before every session so your brain has a target. These are not extreme measures. They are the basic infrastructure for doing the kind of thinking a tech program actually demands.

Focus is not a personality trait you either have or do not have. It is a condition you build by making it easier to concentrate than to escape. Engineer your environment first, and the deep work will follow.

Tips to Remember

  • Use a website blocker like Cold Turkey or Freedom for every focused session — remove the option, do not rely on willpower
  • Study in a space associated with work — your bed is not that space
  • Write down one specific goal before every session so your brain has a clear target
  • Start with your hardest task first while your focus is at its sharpest
  • Build your focus window gradually — if 20 minutes is your limit right now, that is fine, grow it over weeks
